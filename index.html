About Acupuncture

Personalised medical acupuncture helps your body reset its own healing responses. Instead of one-size-fits-all protocols, each session is tailored to your symptoms, lifestyle, and goals. Many people leave feeling calmer, less tense, and more balanced. While results vary, treatment aims to reduce pain and support both physical and emotional well-being.

How it works

Stimulating nerves in skin and muscle can prompt release of natural pain-relievers (e.g., endorphins, serotonin) and influence how pain signals are processed in the brain and spinal cord.

Benefits & Evidence

Clinical research supports short-term relief for lower-back pain, knee pain, neck pain, and certain headaches (migraines, tension-type). It has also been shown to help reduce nausea in specific situations. Evidence varies by condition, and improvement isn't guaranteed.

  • Pain management: back, knee, neck
  • Headaches: migraine, tension-type
  • Nausea: selected contexts (e.g., some postoperative/therapy settings)

Why Choose a Medical Acupuncturist?

Choosing a medical acupuncturist means you're working with a healthcare professional trained in both conventional medicine and acupuncture. They understand your medical history, test results, and medications, and can coordinate care with your general practitioner if needed.

While acupuncture can relieve symptoms and improve quality of life, it is not a substitute for treatment of serious underlying conditions. It should be used alongside, not instead of, standard medical care.

A Blend of Tradition and Modern Science

  1. c. 3300 BCE

    Prehistoric hints (Ötzi)

    Prehistoric hints (Ötzi)
    Tattoos on Ötzi cluster near points later used in acupuncture.
  2. c. 200 BCE–100 CE

    Ancient China texts

    Ancient China texts
    Compilation of foundational works (e.g., Huangdi Neijing) formalised theory and needling practice.
  3. 20th c.

    Western interest

    Western interest
    Public interest surged after 1971 reporting from China; research activity followed.
  4. 1990s–2000s

    Research + integration

    Research + integration
    Major reviews (e.g., NIH 1997) identified areas of benefit and priorities for better trials.
  5. Today (UK)

    Selective NHS use

    Selective NHS use
    Sometimes available on the NHS; NICE (2021) recommends acupuncture for chronic primary pain.

From prehistoric hints and classical Chinese texts to a 20th-century revival and modern evidence reviews, acupuncture has moved from tradition into a selective, research-informed role in UK care - sometimes available on the NHS and recommended by NICE for chronic primary pain.
